Pair Draw

An icebreaker activity where pairs draw a picture together without talking, promoting non-verbal communication and collaboration. This exercise encourages creativity and shared understanding.

Activity Structure​

Pair Draw typically involves two participants sitting side-by-side with a shared piece of paper and a single drawing utensil. One participant initiates the drawing with a simple shape or line, then passes the utensil to their partner, who adds to the drawing without any verbal communication. This process continues back and forth for a set time period (typically 5-10 minutes), resulting in a collaborative drawing created entirely through non-verbal cues and shared artistic intent.

Learning Objectives​

Pair Draw primarily aims to enhance non-verbal communication skills and promote shared understanding within pairs or teams. Participants learn to interpret subtle cues from their partner's drawing style, line weight, and spatial arrangements, developing a deeper sensitivity to unspoken intentions and emotions. The activity also highlights the challenges of collaborative creation without explicit communication, underscoring the importance of clear communication protocols in more complex collaborative tasks.

Facilitation Insights​

Effective facilitation of Pair Draw involves establishing clear constraints that maximize the learning benefits while minimizing potential frustration. Facilitators typically provide simple starting prompts or themes to guide the drawing process without over-constraining creativity. The activity works best when participants have diverse artistic backgrounds, as the differences in style and technique create richer opportunities for non-verbal interpretation. Debriefing discussions after the activity often reveal surprising insights about communication styles and shared assumptions within the group.

Facilitator Guide​

Preparation Checklist​

  • Prepare drawing prompts (optional but recommended)
  • Arrange seating for pairs to sit comfortably side-by-side
  • Test activity timing with a sample group if possible

Implementation Steps​

  1. Setup (5 minutes):

    • Arrange participants in pairs
    • Distribute one sheet of paper and one pen/marker to each pair
    • Explain the activity rules: no talking, one person draws at a time, partners must take turns

  2. Optional: Provide a Theme (1 minute):

    • Suggest a drawing theme (e.g., "Your ideal pair programming workspace" or "A metaphor for collaboration")
    • Alternatively, let pairs choose their own starting point

  3. Drawing Activity (7-10 minutes):

    • Signal the start of the activity
    • Monitor pairs to ensure turn-taking is happening
    • Give a "halfway" time warning
    • Signal when 1 minute remains

  4. Pair Reflection (3 minutes):

    • Ask pairs to discuss their drawing experience with each other
    • Have them identify challenges and successful moments

  5. Group Debrief (10-15 minutes):

    • Facilitate full group discussion using debriefing questions
    • Draw connections to pair programming concepts

Group Size​

  • Optimal: 4-20 participants (2-10 pairs)
  • For larger groups: Consider having some pairs observe others, then rotate

Common Challenges and Solutions​

ChallengeSolution
Participant dominanceEnforce strict turn-taking with timed intervals
Artistic anxietyEmphasize that artistic quality is irrelevant; focus on the process
Side conversationsGently remind participants about the no-talking rule
Pairs finishing earlyHave prompt cards for additional drawing challenges
Reluctance to participateStart with simple shapes rather than complex drawings

Materials​

Required Items​

  • One sheet of paper per pair (A4 or larger)
  • One pen, marker, or pencil per pair (colored markers add interest)
  • Timer or stopwatch
  • Whiteboard or flipchart for debriefing notes

Optional Materials​

  • Theme cards or drawing prompt cards
  • Gallery wall space to display completed drawings
  • Post-it notes for written reflections

Room Setup​

  • Tables or flat surfaces for drawing
  • Pairs should sit side-by-side, not across from each other
  • Arrange seating to minimize distractions from other pairs
  • Ensure adequate lighting for drawing

Debriefing Questions​

  1. Process Reflection:

    • "What was most challenging about creating something without verbal communication?"
    • "What strategies did you develop to understand your partner's intentions?"

  2. Connection to Pair Programming:

    • "How does this experience relate to the driver/navigator roles in pair programming?"
    • "What communication elements from this activity could improve your technical pairing?"

  3. Collaboration Insights:

    • "Did you ever feel frustrated when your partner took the drawing in an unexpected direction?"
    • "How did you balance your own creative vision with your partner's contributions?"

  4. Application:

    • "What one communication insight from this activity will you apply to your next pair programming session?"
    • "How might explicit communication protocols improve both drawing and programming collaborations?"

Follow-up Activities​

  • Have pairs swap drawings and try to interpret the story or meaning
  • Create a "communication checklist" based on insights from the activity
  • In later pair programming sessions, reference back to this experience when communication challenges arise
